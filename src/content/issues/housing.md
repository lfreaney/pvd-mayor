  Providence faces a severe and worsening affordability crisis. The median asking rent hit $2,145 in December 2024, up 12.6% year-over-year — the largest increase of any major U.S. metro. A household earning the city median income of $66,772 can afford roughly $1,675/month at the standard 30% threshold. The gap is $470/month and widening. Rhode Island also has the lowest per-capita home construction rate in the country.

The housing debate in this race gets framed as Smiley (supply) vs. Morales (rent stabilization) — as if these are mutually exclusive. They are not. The research supports both instruments simultaneously, with different timeframes: stabilization to address near-term displacement, supply investment for long-term structural undersupply.

Neither candidate has made this case. Smiley treats supply as sufficient. Morales treats stabilization as sufficient. Both positions are analytically incomplete.

The most important thing neither candidate has addressed: Providence's rent surge is partly driven by Boston-adjacent remote worker migration — a demand shock that no local policy instrument can easily counter.

The legal vulnerability of Morales's central promise is real and underreported. Rhode Island's home rule framework may not give Providence the authority to implement and enforce a rent cap without state enabling legislation. If Morales wins on this promise and the policy is struck down, his first year starts with a major defeat.
