  Providence's neighborhoods are experiencing differentiated development pressure. The East Side, College Hill, and downtown have seen strong investment. South Providence, Olneyville, the West End, and Broad Street corridor face different pressures: rising rents displacing long-term residents, changing commercial character, and underinvestment in physical infrastructure. These neighborhoods are predominantly lower-income and communities of color. The displacement dynamic is related to but distinct from the housing affordability crisis — it is about community stability, cultural continuity, and who gets to stay in the city.

Neighborhood displacement is the human face of the housing crisis — slower moving, less visible in aggregate statistics, and more culturally consequential. When a neighborhood loses its long-term residents, it loses social networks, cultural institutions, small businesses, and the community memory that makes it livable.

South Providence, Olneyville, and the West End are the most vulnerable neighborhoods. They have the highest rent burden, the most turnover in rental housing, and the least protection from displacement. They are also where Providence's immigrant communities, working-class families, and cultural institutions are most concentrated.

Morales's framing here is right and his endorsements reflect genuine community trust. The gap is specificity — what exactly does anti-displacement policy look like, what does it cost, and what state authority does the city need to implement it?

Smiley's record is maintenance of existing conditions, not proactive protection. That is an honest characterization of what three years of fiscal discipline looks like in neighborhoods under pressure.
