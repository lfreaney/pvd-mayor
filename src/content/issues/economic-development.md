context: >
  Providence has seen significant development activity in the Jewelry District, the 195 District, and parts of downtown. The question is not whether development is happening but who benefits. Community benefits agreements (CBAs) require developers to provide local hiring, affordable units, or community investment in exchange for city approvals. Tax increment financing (TIF) and other incentives have been used to attract development but are controversial when they reduce near-term tax revenue. Small businesses, particularly in immigrant communities, have limited access to city support programs.

Economic development is where the candidates' underlying philosophies are clearest. Smiley sees the mayor as a facilitator of private investment. Morales sees the mayor as a negotiator on behalf of residents.

Both positions contain truth. Providence needs private investment — the city cannot develop its way to fiscal health without growing the tax base. But investment without accountability produces the pattern we've seen: downtown revitalization that raises rents citywide while leaving neighborhood commercial corridors and small businesses behind.

The 195 District is the most concrete near-term decision. Remaining parcels represent the city's last significant landholding in a high-value area. How those parcels are developed — what uses, what affordability requirements, what community benefits — is a direct mayoral decision the next administration will make.

Neither candidate has published a specific vision for the remaining 195 parcels. That is a gap worth pressing.
