Providence's cultural sector is one of its most distinctive assets and most underexploited policy levers. The city has a higher concentration of working artists, designers, and creative workers than almost any comparably-sized American city. That concentration drives tourism, attracts businesses, and shapes Providence's identity nationally.

Yet neither candidate has articulated a serious creative economy strategy. The closest Smiley comes is stewardship of the 195 District. The closest Morales comes is a general anti-austerity posture that would theoretically benefit arts funding.

The missing frame: culture is economic infrastructure. A city that loses its artists to rent pressure loses the thing that makes it distinctive. Anti-displacement policy for artists and creative spaces — deed restrictions, subsidized studio space, zoning protections — is a concrete mayoral action that neither candidate has proposed.

The 195 District is the most concrete opportunity. How creative uses are prioritized in remaining parcels is a decision the next mayor will influence directly.
